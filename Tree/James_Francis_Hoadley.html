The Hoadley Family

James Francis Hoadley

8. JAMES FRANCIS HOADLEY (Elias #2, Thomas #1), born 16 August, 1831 in Hartland, Vt.; died 17 August, 1891 in Cloverdale Calif.; married 31 October, 1852 in Spring- field, Penna., HARRIET CELESTIAL APPLEBY of Springfield, born 8 July, 1832; died 13 September, 1913 in Cloverdale.

James Francis Hoadley and his wife set out in the spring of 1853 for California across the plains. After meeting with many hardships, they arrived in Shasta County on October 6th and settled in the town of Lewiston, Trinity County, where he established a trading post. He packed his goods on mules from Shasta over the Trinity Mountains. In 1855 he closed out his business and built a sawmill and toll road. While there he held the offices of town supervisor and justice of the peace a number of terms, and was postmaster for four years. In the fall of 1872 he sold out his interest in Lewiston and moved to Cloverdale, Sonoma County, where he engaged in the hardware business. He sold it in 1875 and made his first visit to his native town in Vermont. He returned to Cloverdale and again engaged in business with which he was occupied until his death. He was a justice of the peace and a notary public.

Hoadley, Page #464: A native of Windor County, Vermont, born 16 August, 1831, where he was educated and resided until 19 years of age, when he moved to Erie County, Pennsylvania, and remained about two years.

3 November 1852, he was married to Carrie (incorrect-should be-) Harriet C. Appleby, of Springfield, Erie County, Pennsylvania, and moved to Jefferson County, Wisconsin, where he remained until the spring of 1853. He then set out for California. Crossing the plains he arrived in Shasta County, October 6th of the same year. Remaining here a short time, he settled in the town of Lewiston, Trinity County, where he established a Trading Post, and packed his goods from Shasta over Trinity Mountain on mules until 1858, then he closed that out and built a saw mill and carried on a small farm. In the summer of 1865 helped to build a toll road, from Tower House in Shasta County to Lewiston. After about two years he became the owner of the toll road, and then completed it to Weaverville in Trinity County. While here he held the office of Supervisor two terms, and that of Justice of Peace one term; was Postmaster for four years. In the fall of 1872, he sold out his interest here and came to Sonoma County settling in Cloverdale. Here he formed a co-partnership with Isaac E. Shaw in a general hardware and grocery store, which lasted until January 1875 when he withdrew from the firm, and made a trip to his native State, leaving June 13th and returning the following September. He then purchased the lumber yard of Leander Shores, which business he has continued ever since. In 1876 he built the Centennial Warehouse at Cloverdale, since which time he has added and carried on a forwarding and commission business.

THE CLOVERDALE REVEILLE. This newspaper made its appearance in the first week of October 1879. W.S. Walker being the publisher and proprietor. The Cloverdale News was started in November 1876, by the same gentleman, but in the following April he disposed of his interest to J. F. HOADLEY, who continued its publication until the spring of the present year, when he moved the office to Santa Rosa, running it in the interest of the new Constitution Party, at which place he suspended its publication in September 1879.

THE CLOVERDALE NEWS This newspaper was started in November 1876 by W. S. Walker; after issuing several numbers, Mr. Walker sold his interest to J.F. Hoadley, and the paper is now under the editorial control of J. F. Hoadley, Jr. Mr. Hoadley is young in the business, but makes a very readable paper, which will bear its full share in the future development of the interests of the city of Cloverdale and the surrounding country.

There are a number of very neat residences in town, among the best are the houses of Isaac E. Shaw, H. Kier, James Francis Hoadley, and the pioneer, J. A. Kleiser.

Fraternally Mr. J. F. Hoadley was a member of Cloverdale Lodge #32,A.0.U.W.- The Lodge of Ancient Order of United Workmen of Cloverdale, which was granted its charter 3 May 1878. He held the office of Receiver for two terms, while his son, E. A. Hoadley was Financier. Later Mr. Hoadley held the esteemed position of P.M.W., as head of his Lodge.

When Mr.J. F. Hoadley arrived in California, he received a Patent on land located in what was then known as Louis town, later changed to Lewiston, Trinity County. This patent was not recorded at the County Seat in Weaverville until after Mr. Hoadley had sold the property and removed himself to Cloverdale.

The Hoadley Toll Road from Tower House to Lewiston, is now known as the Turnpike Road, and is kept open in the summer only by the Forestry Dept. There are three Peaks on the southern end of the Trinity Alps near Lewiston that have been named after James Francis Hoadley, and are indicated on all maps as the HOADLEY PEAKS. They are 4584 feet in height. Mr. Hoadley in his latter years in Cloverdale, in addition to his business enterprise, imported horses from France.

There is a Plot Plan of the City of Cloverdale, circa 1884, hanging on the wall of the City Hall, which depicts the Hoadley home,the Hoadley Block, the Hoadley Warehouse (still standing and in operation), and the Hoadley Commission Warehouse.

Mr. Hoadley died at the age of 60 years, from paint poisoning, and is buried in the family plot in the Riverside Cemetery, Cloverdale. His wife Harriet Celestial Hoadley, his brother De. George Clinton Hoadley, daughters Harriet Mehitable Hoadley Whitney and Ida Louise Hoadley Humbert, and son George Henry Hoadley are buried there also.
